Education and Abstract Concepts

Education often deals with facts, dates, and hard concepts. These are subjects like history and english which are difficult to learn at times but more rigid in structure. There are also abstract concepts, things like math and science which are more flexible, intangible, and often harder to understand. This is because you cannot see the way atoms move and honestly probably have a hard time visualizing atoms to begin with. Abstract concepts would be easier to understand if you could see them and work directly with them. VR allows this to occur, for the first time students would be able to see and play with atoms.

According to a Tech Crunch Article, "When Virtual Reality Meets Education", most VR for education has been focused into the hard sciences like anatomy, astronomy, and geology.1 This makes sense as science can benefit greatly from AR and VR technology. A study done by Chris Bryne also talkes a bit about abstract learning. It talks of what it would be like to learn of electron fields while being able to see atoms and electrons float in space before you.2 To be able to view small and large worlds, dying stars, and frictionless collisions.

There are many ways that VR and AR could improve abstract learning or make it cheaper to run certain programs. Art is not abstract in and of itself however it is far more subjective than most other subject matters and requires a lot of material. AR and VR could help bring painting and drawing from the physical world to the digital world. This would be cheaper to fund and in the long term a new way of storing and creating art.

It also has potential in math classes because you can look at three dimensional cubes, two dimensional triangle, and almost anything else without too much effort. Sometimes it is hard to imagine how different things work in geometry, but this would make learning easier by places the imagine the students need to see directly in front of them.

Education is full of abstract learning and difficult concepts. Technology has the potential to change how we learn, the potential to make it easier to grasp these ideas. VR and AR is the next step to improving learning for everyone.
